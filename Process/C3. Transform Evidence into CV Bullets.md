## Purpose
Transform the approved evidence from Step [[C2. Map JD Requirements to Supporting Evidence]] into concise, impactful CV bullet points. These bullets must remain truthful to the candidate’s actual experience while being written in language that improves both **ATS compatibility** and **Talent Acquisition appeal**.

The goal is to produce bullet points that:

- Clearly demonstrate fulfilment of the Job Description requirements.
- Use strong action verbs and, wherever possible, include measurable results or impact.
- Maintain a consistent, professional tone aligned with senior-level roles.

---

## A. Execution Sequence

1. Use the **final cleaned C2 export** as the single source of truth. Only use evidence that was marked as approved (Green / final set) in C2.
2. For each requirement, review the associated evidence (Reference + Original Text).
3. Transform each piece of evidence into one or more CV bullet points following the [[C3. Transform Evidences into CV Bullets#B. CV Bullet Writing Principles|CV Bullet Writing Principles]].
4. When one requirement has multiple pieces of evidence, create separate bullets (or group them logically when appropriate).
5. Add the resulting bullet(s) in a new column called **CV Bullet**.
6. Review all bullets for consistency, impact, and truthful representation of the candidate’s experience.
7. Produce the final output table.

---

## B. CV Bullet Writing Principles

When transforming evidence into CV bullets, apply the following principles:

### 1. Truthfulness First

- When transforming evidence into bullets, use the `Original Text` column from the final  [[C2. Map JD Requirements to Supporting Evidence]] export as the primary source for the bullet content. This ensures truthfulness. 
- Use the `Requirement Skills` column to guide the language and focus of the bullet, especially when writing the bracketed skill tag at the end.
- The `Reference` column indicates the exact source of the evidence in the Profile Reference Workbook.
- Avoid significantly rephrasing or generalising the evidence just to make it sound more impressive or to fit Job Description language.
- If the original evidence is specific, the bullet should reflect that specificity. If it is more general, do not artificially narrow or broaden it.
- Do not invent achievements, exaggerate scope, or add elements that are not supported by the source material.
- If the original evidence is indirect or partial, the bullet should still reflect reality (you may soften the claim rather than overstate it).


### 2. Natural Integration of Job Description Language

- Incorporate relevant keywords and phrasing from the Job Description **only when they fit naturally** within the bullet.
- Do not force Job Description keywords into the bullet. If incorporating them requires changing the meaning or flow of the original evidence, leave them out. The bracketed tag at the end is the appropriate place to reference relevant skills.
- The objective is **compatibility**, not keyword stuffing.
- The bullet must still read naturally and professionally.


### 3. Strong Action Verbs

- Start every bullet with a strong, specific action verb (e.g., _Led_, _Designed_, _Implemented_, _Coordinated_, _Built_, _Delivered_, _Transformed_).
- Avoid weak verbs such as _Helped_, _Assisted_, _Worked on_, or _Involved in_.

### 4. Results and Impact

- Include measurable results, scale, or business impact **when they exist** in the Original Text.
- Do not invent or exaggerate results that are not supported by the evidence.
- If no clear result exists in the evidence, focus on the **action and scope** instead of forcing a weak outcome.
- Examples of good additions: timelines, number of stakeholders, countries involved, efficiency gains, regulatory compliance achieved, cost savings, etc.


### 5. Skill Association – Consistent and Non-Forceful Approach

Use the following hierarchy when associating skills with bullets:

**Primary Method (Default):**  
Add skills as **bracketed tags** at the end of the bullet. Use language that is close to the Job Lead’s wording where possible. This is the most consistent and reliable method.

**Secondary Method (Selective Use):**  
Apply **light, selective bold** inline **only** when the skill can be highlighted naturally without requiring changes to the original evidence. 

Do not attempt to force bold inline integration. If the bullet needs to be significantly rephrased to include a skill in bold, use a bracketed tag instead. The goal is to improve human readability, not to artificially insert keywords.

**Rule:** Avoid heavily mixing both methods (bold + bracketed tags) within the same role or section, unless it genuinely improves clarity. Prioritise consistency and readability.

**Example of good application:**

> Acted as a **trusted mediator** between the Management Board and department heads during a politically sensitive transformation, enabling progress despite internal resistance. _(Stakeholder Management, Conflict Mediation)_

**Note on Skill Integration:**
The preferred approach is to use bracketed tags as the main method for linking bullets to skills. Selective bold should only be used sparingly and naturally. The objective is to produce authentic, readable bullets that still support ATS matching through relevant keywords in the bracketed tags.

**Consistency of Bracketed Tags**

Bracketed tags should be kept **short, clear, and consistent** across all bullets. 

- Aim for **1 to 4 words** per tag.
- Use similar phrasing style throughout the CV (e.g., noun-based tags such as *“Stakeholder Management”*, *“Change Adoption”*, *“Risk Monitoring”*).
- Avoid overly long or descriptive tags, as they reduce readability.
- Do not mix very short tags with long, sentence-like tags in the same CV.

Overuse or inconsistent use of bracketed tags can make the CV look cluttered and reduce its professional appearance. When a skill can be naturally integrated into the bullet text using selective bold, prefer that method over adding another bracketed tag.


>[!Note]
The bracketed tag carries **JD-compatible language** (`Requirement Skills`) — your judgment of which Job Lead skill this specific bullet demonstrates. Traceability to your own profile runs through **My Skills**, established at [[C2. Map JD Requirements to Supporting Evidence]] from whichever evidence item (STAR action, responsibility, or bullet) was matched to this requirement — not through a B5-level mapping, and a tag does not need its own `tbl_Skills_Master` entry to be valid. [[C4. Build and Manage the Skills Section]] cross-checks the top Skills List against `tbl_Skills_Master`, `tbl_STAR_Competences` and `tbl_STAR_Attributes`, but only as a fallback ranking source to fill remaining slots — every My Skills value carried on a Keep row is included regardless.

### 6. Conciseness and Readability

- Keep bullets concise and easy to read.
- Remove unnecessary words while preserving meaning and impact.
- Favour clarity and natural flow over trying to include every possible keyword.
- One strong, well-crafted bullet is better than two average ones.

### 7. Multiple Bullets per Requirement (1-to-many)

- When a requirement has multiple strong pieces of evidence, create **separate bullets** rather than combining everything into one long bullet.
- Only combine evidence into one bullet if the pieces are very closely related and the result is still clear and impactful.

## C. Output Format

**Primary Output** An expanded table based on the final C2 export, with one additional column:

**CV Bullet**

**Columns (in exact order):**

Lead · Lead:ID · Requirement Order · Requirement Group · Rank ·  Requirement · Requirement Description · Requirement Skills · My Skills · Reference · Original Text · CV Position · **CV Bullet**

**Rules:**

- One row per approved evidence piece (from the final C2 export).
- The **CV Bullet** column contains the transformed bullet point(s).
- If one evidence piece generates multiple bullets, duplicate the row and add a sequence number (e.g., 1/2, 2/2) in an optional **Bullet Sequence** column.

---

## D. Quality & Consistency Rules

- Only use evidence from the **final cleaned C2 export**. Do not introduce new evidence that was not approved in C2.
- Maintain a consistent tone across all bullets (senior-level, strategic, action-oriented).
- Ensure bullets are **ATS-friendly** (clear keywords, structured, no excessive formatting).
- Balance **JD language** with readability. The bullet should still feel authentic to the candidate’s voice and experience.
- When in doubt between a more impressive phrasing and strict truthfulness, **choose truthfulness**.
- After writing all bullets, perform a final consistency check across the entire table (verb variety, length, impact level).

---

### Notes & Quality Rules

- C3 builds directly on the **final approved evidence** from C2. Any evidence that was removed during deduplication in C2 should not be used here.
- The quality of the CV bullets directly impacts both ATS scoring and the impression made on Talent Acquisition and Hiring Managers.
- Strong bullets are **specific, results-oriented, and truthful** — not generic or exaggerated.
- C3 is a critical bridge between the analytical work done in C2 and the actual CV tailoring that will happen in later steps.
- **Truthfulness**: Base the **content** of the bullet on the `Original Text` column from C2 (this is the actual evidence).
- Avoid forced rephrasing: Do not significantly alter the Original Text from C2 in order to insert skills, keywords, or to create bold inline text. Bullets should remain close to the source evidence. When in doubt, prioritise truthfulness and natural phrasing over stylistic optimisation.
- **Language alignment**: Use the `Requirement Skills` column to choose appropriate **keywords and phrasing** for the bracketed tag at the end of the bullet.
